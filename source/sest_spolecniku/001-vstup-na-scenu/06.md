# You Don’t Need to Insult Me Twice

One reason bronze citizens rarely advanced was the challenge system — designed to ensure that only those willing to *give more than expected* could rise among the elite.

Children learned this early in school, drilled by teachers explaining why the current society was the best possible. In a way, it was a polished revival of medieval dueling traditions, elevated into romantic ideals.

Naturally, the goal wasn’t to eliminate candidates. The offended party had the right to issue a challenge, while the challenged party chose the type of duel. It didn’t have to be physical — armed or unarmed — but could be strategic games like go or chess, believed to develop intellectual ability.

However, if the challenged party lost in a duel they considered their *forte*, they suffered a severe loss of face. That meant demotion in the corporate hierarchy, possibly even loss of citizenship status. Corporations took duels very seriously and did not tolerate defeat among their employees. On the other hand, they actively rewarded victories against rival corporations, since those increased prestige.

Becoming a silver citizen meant reaching and maintaining the peak of physical and mental capability. A bronze citizen who performed excellently at work but failed to score in the challenge system had no hope of advancement.

“You…!”

The woman stared at Xavier’s silver badge in shock. She didn’t intend to apologize — instead she grew angrier.

“I should have known I’d run into a hired thug-provoker. Has Miss Lydia sunk so low? Hiring a pauper like you? Is she trying to insult me?”

Xavier didn’t bother explaining that he didn’t know Miss Lydia. He simply gestured politely. Denying it would be pointless — she wouldn’t believe him anyway. And one of his professions wasn’t far from what she accused him of.

In short, anyone could hire a provoker to defeat an opponent in a duel. The shame always fell on the loser. If the provoker won, the client humiliated the rival. If the provoker lost, the client didn’t care — the fee was the same.

If a provoker lost more often than he won, he became a “shmuck-provoker,” losing clients. He might be annoying, but no one took him seriously.

“Shmuck-provoker,” Xavier smiled tiredly. “You don’t need to insult me twice. Just pick one.”

“Hmph.” She snorted. “Too bad you didn’t check who Miss Lydia sent you against. Young Alfred is not someone you can provoke. I’m his personal assistant.”

From her tone, Xavier guessed Alfred must be a gold citizen — only golds inspired such reverence in silvers.

“Haven’t decided yet?” he asked sweetly, pointing at her headband. “Or haven’t you figured out who you’re dealing with?”

He was certain she was stalling, waiting for her AI to pull up whatever data it could find on her opponent. Officially she shouldn’t have access to his statistics, but unofficially several companies sold such information. It would be an insult to the mysterious Alfred if he hadn’t secured at least one dueling database for his assistant.

“You’re not worth the effort,” she snapped. But for a moment she looked confused.  
If Xavier excelled at anything, it was flying under the radar. His profession required him to stay off such lists, and much of his income went into erasing those records.

“It seems you’re nobody worth knowing,” she concluded when her AI failed. In a way, she was right — a true expert provoker lived off reputation, not anonymity.

“So?”

“I don’t have time for you,” she barked. “We’ll settle this quickly. Fistfight. Here and now. Do you agree?”

Without waiting for his answer, she turned to the pale clerk and gestured for him to follow her outside.

“You there! You’ll be my second. And stop trembling. I don’t expect miracles.”

In physical duels where one party might lose consciousness, the second had the right to surrender on behalf of the duelist.

“I forbid you to interfere,” she said, stripping down to sports underwear.  
Her muscles had a reddish tint — a sign of regular use of supertonic drinks. She looked graceful and feminine, but Xavier had no doubt she could kill an ox with one punch. Supertonics and subdermal impact implants made her a dangerous opponent.

*Too bad she didn’t choose chess,* Xavier thought bitterly. *I might have had a chance.*

“Hurry up,” she urged, reading his sour expression. “Young Alfred hates waiting. Where’s your second? Pick someone and let’s begin.”

“One moment. I think he’s already on his way.”

From the Game Centre across the street, a familiar bearded figure emerged. Even from a distance, Xavier saw Medard’s barely contained excitement.

*Success?* Xavier waved.  
But as soon as Medard reached the electronics store and saw Xavier and the unknown woman preparing for what was clearly a formal duel, his excitement evaporated.

“What happened?” he asked nervously.

“That lady needs a lesson,” Xavier said loudly, then whispered:  
“That’s why I’ll let her beat me. I haven’t slept in days — I can barely see. When you get the chance, throw in the towel.”

“Of course… I’m sorry, ma’am,” Medard said timidly, then pointed to a grassy patch.  
“Our side does not agree to fight on pavement. We’ll move to the lawn.”

“What? Are you afraid?”

“Uh… well…”

“Of course we’re afraid,” Xavier shouted boastfully. “I’m afraid I’ll hurt you.”

He stripped down to his trousers and removed his shoes. On the grassy patch he began stretching, ignoring her impatient huffing.  
As expected, once word spread that two silver citizens were dueling, people abandoned the Game Centre queue and gathered around the improvised ring.

“Look at her muscles. That guy doesn’t stand a chance.”

“You think?”

“Absolutely. Two-to-one odds on the Lady.”

“In that case, a thousand on the Guy.”

“Deal.”

Bored onlookers seized the chance to place bets.  
The silver woman frowned at the nickname “Lady,” but she was smart enough not to protest. She wasn’t dealing with a lone clerk now, but with an anonymous crowd that had long accepted they had nothing to lose.

“Seems even the plastic-tags don’t believe in you,” she mocked. “And you look so much like them.”

“Hey, friend,” Xavier called to the self-appointed bookmaker. “Can I bet on myself?”

“Why not,” the man smirked, “if you don’t mind losing your money.”

He didn’t look trustworthy, but he boasted loudly:  
“Don’t worry, folks. I’ve got the best reputation and the best odds in town.”

“Here’s ten thousand,” Xavier created a virtual banknote and sent it to Medard.  
“Go place the bet. Make sure it goes through the system. If I somehow win, I want my double.”

The bookmaker tapped his chest.  
“I’m an honest man, Mr Guy. If you win, I’ll pay you to the last coin — and eat those socks stinking next to your shoes.”

“I’ll hold you to that, friend.”

The crowd burst into laughter, clearly siding with Xavier.  
No one wanted to cheer for the arrogant corporate assistant.

“Pathetic,” she sneered. “Begging for sympathy wherever you can. They pegged you right — a loser without dignity.”

Xavier shrugged and finished stretching.  
Winning the crowd wasn’t much, but she clearly struggled with the disrespectful comments thrown her way.  
If the crowd turned against her, she might slip.

“I placed the bet,” Medard said when he returned. “Maybe I’m wrong, but you don’t look confident.”

“I’m not,” Xavier smirked. “But betting on her wouldn’t fly — and her odds are terrible.”

“I put a thousand on you,” Medard added. “You have to win now — for me.”

Xavier blinked.  
He barely knew Medard, yet the man had made a bold choice.  
More than anything, it looked like he wanted to encourage his new friend.

“No wonder the corporations don’t want you,” Xavier said, patting his back.  
“You make foolish, sentimental investments.”

Medard didn’t reply and went to negotiate duel conditions with the trembling clerk.  
The poor man had never been in such a situation.

Bronze and plastic citizens did duel, but rarely — not for lack of courage, but because they lacked access to advanced medical care.  
A broken arm or open wound meant weeks of suffering.  
For silver and gold citizens, it was an hour-long procedure at a private clinic.

Eventually the seconds agreed, and Xavier’s opponent stepped into the ring.

“Xantipa Miller, personal assistant at **Asphalt-Travel**, multiple fistfight champion. Second-category citizen.”

Xavier followed suit:  
“Xavier Valehart, second-category citizen, freelance. Multiple champion in insomnia.”

Another wave of laughter erupted.

Xantipa scowled.  
“You’re a clown. This isn’t a comedy contest. You might have a chance there — but here and now…”

She lunged forward — so fast the crowd gasped.  
Some regretted mocking her; she displayed agility usually seen only in viral clips.

“Oh boy,” someone groaned. “The Guy is done for.”

And Xavier was the first to agree with the anonymous voice.
