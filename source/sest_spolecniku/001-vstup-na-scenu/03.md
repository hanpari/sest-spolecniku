# Intellectual Delight Beyond Price?

“A message? Absolutely not,” the doctor said, pointing at the motto hanging above the door of the admissions office.

> I do not speak, I serve.

“Becoming part of the Game means keeping secrets. That applies to everyone here.”

When the doctor’s gesture included **Xavier Valehart**, Xavier smirked.  
“I understand. No messages.”

“Exactly. Whatever we discuss here must never reach the public. By entering this room, you agreed to confidentiality.”

On the bearded man’s leaflet — the one Xavier had skimmed earlier — a long list of binding conditions was printed in tiny letters, likely including this one. Yet another reason to avoid entanglement with the private sector. Corporate lawyers were not paid to overlook minor infractions. Whoever broke the rules paid for it.

“Let’s continue,” Xavier said, feeling bitterly that he had stepped into a carefully laid trap. “Since I’m already here, let’s finish what needs finishing.”

The older nurse, who had been on edge the whole time, sensed her moment had come. With the triumphant expression of a shepherd welcoming a lost sheep, she sat down in the swivel chair.

“Name?”

“Xavier Valehart.”

“Age?”

“Thirty‑six. Listen, nurse, we can skip this,” Xavier said, handing her the silver badge he had removed from his neck. “Here’s my identifier. I’m sure your systems know more about me than I do.”

“Do you consent to the processing of your data?”

“Do I have a choice?” Xavier replied vaguely. There were countless tricks to outsmart corporate lawyers. If the nurse used his badge without asking for explicit consent, the contract between him and the Game was technically challengeable.

It was more than likely that the entire conversation was being recorded — otherwise the doctor might have behaved differently, less guarded and mysterious. If Xavier ever decided to challenge the corporation behind the Game, he would have a slim but real chance of success.

A mistake of this kind, if discovered, would mean the end of the nurse’s corporate career — and given her age, the end of her bronze badge as well. Xavier glanced at the doctor and noticed he was about to warn her. When he caught Xavier’s stare, he changed his mind.

“It’s always best,” the doctor said, “to avoid misunderstandings between us, Mr Valehart. I’m Doctor Blackheart.”

He did not introduce the two nurses. If things ever came to a breaking point, he was clearly prepared to sacrifice them.  
*Why does he care so much about staying on my good side?* Xavier wondered. *Even if he’s paid per contract, surely there aren’t so few candidates capable of entering?*

“We’ll be in regular contact,” the doctor added. “You should know that the sooner you enter the Game properly, the better for you. You’ll gain a very exclusive position. Please, don’t underestimate anything.”

*Don’t underestimate anything?*  
Xavier began to understand he had stepped into a web of subtle hints — a favourite pastime among those bound by secrecy but desperate to warn the other side.

“I assume the Game places high demands on its users.”

The doctor shook his head. “Of course it does. But you don’t need to worry. Your health won’t be endangered. Quite the opposite.”

*Then why do we need to stay in contact?* Xavier wondered. His assumption that the doctor wanted to earn points through frequent check‑ups was proving wrong.

“You mustn’t think of the Game as a leisure pastime, Xavier. May I call you Xavier?”

“Go on, doctor,” Xavier nodded. He remembered the people in the queue talking about money.  
*What is this about? Does he want me to buy virtual items?*  
The doctor seemed increasingly nervous about Xavier’s lack of understanding.

“The Game offers a vast unexplored world, Xavier,” he said. “You may be surprised, but valuable surprises can appear. Isn’t it said that discovering new things is an intellectual delight beyond price?”

**Valuable surprises** and **intellectual delight beyond price**?  
Key phrases pointing to profit — something connected to what could be found inside the Game.  
*Items or information?*  
If the doctor had no access to the Game itself — at least not yet — what use would virtual items be to him?

“It seems the Game fascinates you, doctor.”

“Immensely,” the doctor said with relief. “Believe me, it’s such a revolutionary endeavour that we can’t even imagine its full reach. Truly, don’t underestimate anything,” he repeated firmly.

“In that case, I hope you’ll give me a few tips gratis. I’d rather not get burned. You seem like an insider.”

“Not too much of an insider,” the doctor sighed. “You’ll have the chance to learn more on the front line.”

*So he wants information!* Xavier realised.  
*And those information are worth bending corporate rules. What could be so miraculous about the Game that he’d risk his career for it?*

It was clear neither nurse had any idea a secret agreement was unfolding. The older nurse diligently filled out forms on the computer, while the younger prepared a massive padded chair tangled with cables and sensors.

“Mr Valehart,” she said nervously when she finished. “May I ask you? You’ll need to undress to your underwear.”

“Of course.”

In an age where people spent most of their time in virtual reality, imperfect human bodies had become taboo. Few would willingly undress before strangers, and few could tolerate such a sight without feeling queasy.

It was hard to say who felt more embarrassed — Xavier, who had to undress, or the young nurse, likely too accustomed to the flawless bodies of corporate princes and princesses she secretly dreamed about.

“Mr Valehart!” she cried in shock as she attached the first sensors. “You have scars. Why?”

“An accident,” Xavier lied smoothly.

“I didn’t mean how you got them,” she said innocently. “I don’t understand why you didn’t have them removed. You have a silver badge, don’t you?”

In the public eye — especially in the eyes of a bronze‑badge holder — a silver badge was a blessing, a glimpse into the golden world of gods and goddesses. Being a silver citizen and still having scars that might offend the delicate sensibilities of higher beings was practically a mortal sin. Perhaps the young nurse simply couldn’t comprehend why Xavier wouldn’t use every privilege available to him.

“They don’t bother me,” Xavier muttered as he sat in the padded chair. The nurse strapped his ankles and wrists, then his waist, thighs, and arms. In the end, he couldn’t move at all.

“Do you tolerate pain well?” the doctor asked suddenly.

“Pain?”

“I’m afraid the test you’re about to undergo will be a bit unpleasant.”

“You could have warned me earlier,” Xavier smirked. “This way it looks like you waited until I couldn’t escape.”

“People have a complicated relationship with pain,” the doctor said. “They avoid it. It’s not in your interest to avoid our test.”

“In mine or yours?”

“You’ll be grateful.”

It wasn’t hard to recognise the pattern of corporate behaviour. As long as they didn’t have their victims under control, they behaved politely, like ordinary human beings.

Only when given the chance did they prove there was nothing they wouldn’t do for career advancement. That was how they were trained, and that was what was expected of them. Xavier could only blame himself for letting them strap him in. The incident in the corridor should have warned him nothing pleasant awaited him.

Before the doctor could continue, the older nurse — still not finished with her work — cried out in surprise.

“What’s wrong?”

“I can’t access some of Mr Valehart’s records.”

“Let me see. It’s impossible that—”

When the doctor realised she lacked sufficient clearance, he tried logging in under his own name — with the same result.

“Remarkable,” he murmured. “What do you have to say about this, Xavier?”

“Me? Do you really want to know something your superiors didn’t give you access to?”

Xavier wasn’t foolish enough to explain anything. Fear of the unknown carried more weight for this kind of people. Their training required them to suppress curiosity and stay within strict boundaries. Xavier silently pointed at another motto on the wall:

> Do not ask. You know what you need to know.

“But surely you could tell me—”

Not even rigorous training had turned Doctor Blackheart into a perfectly obedient puppet. Xavier gave him credit for that — the doctor tried several times, despite the older nurse’s clear disapproval, to uncover Xavier’s past. When he finally gave up, he signalled the younger nurse to proceed.

“This part will be a little unpleasant,” she warned. “Please try to endure it.”

There was no comforting reassurance that he could stop at any time — and Xavier didn’t expect any. For an ordinary mortal, the next phase would be a shock, but not for him. The chair resembled a luxurious version of the device the military used to test how long a soldier could withstand torture in captivity.

“Go on, nurse,” Xavier growled and bit down on the prepared gag.  
A moment later, the first jolts of pain struck. Hundreds of needles bit into his nerve clusters, leaving no marks — but rationalising pain didn’t help.

“I’ll be projecting simple puzzles,” the nurse explained. “Nothing complicated. You’ll solve them at different levels.”

*Sensitive little thing,* Xavier smirked inwardly.  
He knew the “levels” referred not to puzzle difficulty but to degrees of suffering. If the young nurse couldn’t stomach the sight of old pink scars, the sight of a body twisted in agony would haunt her dreams. Even the older nurse preferred staring at the monitor, while Doctor Blackheart remained coldly detached — perhaps hoping to uncover the secrets of Xavier’s past.
