# It’s Time for a Revolution

When Xavier stepped out of the examination room, he ran into clusters of disappointed candidates loudly dissecting their failures.

“Have you met anyone who actually got inside?”

“I’ll be honest, this is my third time here, and I’m starting to think it’s all a scam.”

“My thoughts exactly. I reported the Game Centre to the police — told them it’s run by a bunch of frauds.”

“Good luck with that, man. I’ve got a friend in the police. Says they get thousands of complaints every day. They’ve been ordered to ignore all of them.”

“We plastic‑tags never get protection. Anyone can take us for a ride.”

“What did they take from you? Half an hour of your precious time? I’m telling you — we have to endure. Persistence is the key.”

The overall mood swung between rebellious frustration and resigned apathy.  
If these people learned that Xavier — a silver citizen — had succeeded, they would likely tear him apart for the mere suspicion that his status gave him an unfair advantage.

“So, friend, how did you do?” someone asked.

“Damn poorly,” Xavier muttered. “You won’t see me here again.”

“You’re right. To hell with the corporations. Jumping when they whistle? Not me.”

“It’s time for a revolution, brothers,” a familiar voice said behind him.  
Xavier had heard revolutionary calls before, but never delivered so timidly.  
It sounded less like defiance and more like a terrified lamb among wolves.

“It’s you? I wouldn’t have expected such bold words from you, brother.”

Xavier turned and recognized the bearded man. The fellow grabbed his arm and pulled him aside, away from the agitated crowd.

“We need to talk,” he whispered. His eyes were red, his expression urgent.  
“Those three thugs are waiting for you. You must avoid them… And may the revolution prevail, brothers!” he added in a barely audible whimper.

“You have to be louder,” Xavier scolded, raising a clenched fist.  
“How long will we let them crap on our heads?” he roared.  
“Plastic‑tags, what do you have to lose? Revolution or death!”

“Please, not so loud,” the bearded man begged. “Someone might hear you.”

“So you prefer revolutions whispered under your breath,” Xavier laughed and let himself be led around the corner.  
As expected, no revolution erupted — the crowd’s chanting fizzled out in awkward silence.  
If street unrest could topple a regime, the authorities would have dealt with it long ago.  
Usually, all it took was to leave the angry citizens alone, and they returned to their lives.

“You’re brave,” the bearded man said, a mix of admiration and horror.  
“Aren’t you afraid the cameras caught you?”

“Don’t be naïve. Next time plan your uprising better. Do you know where the ammunition depots are? Do you have officers on your side? Loyal masses?”

“Please, don’t joke. I just… I don’t know what came over me. A moment of weakness.”

“You realized you have nothing to lose.”

Without asking permission, Xavier reached under the man’s shirt and pulled out the plastic badge he kept hidden — probably out of shame.

“How much time do you have left?” Xavier asked gently. “When was your last vaccination?”

“It’s been years,” the man admitted, crestfallen. “I have two children. I wanted to stay with them until… until I have to go. Getting into the Game seemed like a good idea.”

“But it didn’t work?” Xavier knew the answer but wanted to let him speak.

“I don’t have the courage to go back, friend. The moment I entered the corridor, I felt sick. I understood they didn’t want me. Something pushed me to run.”

“I see.”

Suspecting that the difference in affinity wasn’t as dramatic as Doctor Blackheart claimed, Xavier asked for details.  
It seemed the deciding factor wasn’t innate ability but the man’s deeply ingrained sense of inferiority — the belief that corporations didn’t want him — which made him give up the moment he felt resistance.

“But you,” the man said suddenly, turning to Xavier, “you came out after an hour. I returned as soon as I saw those three looking for you. I couldn’t miss you.”

He looked at Xavier as if the stranger’s success could compensate for his own failure.  
“I knew you were the type who pushes through. You’re made of different stuff than me. Always the winner, never the loser, right?”

A bit embarrassed by the praise, Xavier focused on the plastic badge still in his hand.

“Listen, Medard,” he said after reading the name. “I appreciate that you waited to warn me. That’s more than anyone else would’ve done. The rest would tear me apart for my silver badge.”

“I’m just a weakling,” Medard said miserably. “Not good enough for the corporations. But still…”  
He looked at Xavier with a spark of hope.  
“Maybe a small hint. A nudge in the right direction.”

“The moment you get inside, you’re bound to secrecy. I’m expected not to reveal a single word. Otherwise…”

“I understand. Of course.”

Knowing Xavier could get in trouble with corporate lawyers, Medard immediately stopped pressing.

“You really aren’t cut out for corporate work,” Xavier laughed and patted his back.  
“First rule of corporate employees: go after what you want. You said it yourself.”

“Nonsense. You’d get in trouble, and I might not even benefit.”

“Caring about other people’s problems is a sin. Selfishness benefits society.”

“Bullshit!”

“That’s the spirit. Shake on it.”

After they shook hands, Xavier introduced himself and quietly explained how to reach the admissions office and what to do.  
Medard was too fascinated to interrupt — overwhelmed by Xavier’s complete disregard for corporate legal threats.

Still, he hesitated.  
“Even if I get inside, I’m not sure I can handle the pain.”

Xavier had no comforting answer.  
Instead, they returned to the Game Centre and joined the queue.  
When Medard finally went in — trembling from head to toe — Xavier headed to the nearest electronics store.

In the glass display he saw rows of gaming devices, but none related to the Game.  
Above the entrance glowed a sign:

> We do not carry the Game. Please visit your nearest Game Centre.

That didn’t stop a dozen customers from bombarding the exhausted clerk with the same questions.  
When the man in the white shirt saw Xavier, he greeted him with a wilted smile.

“What can I get you, sir? If you’re looking for anything related to the Game, I’m afraid we don’t carry it yet — nor any accessories.”

Instead of answering, Xavier showed him his AICHAT glasses.  
“Someone suggested I should replace this model. Do you have glasses with artificial intelligence?”

Even that question didn’t brighten the clerk’s mood.

“You want glasses specifically, sir? We have some older models, but no one buys them anymore. Customers prefer OR.”

He tapped the black plastic headband with a tiny diode above his forehead.

“OR?”

As he aged, Xavier stopped following new tech.

“Organic Interface,” the clerk explained. “It sends signals directly to your optic nerves. I highly recommend trying it.”

“Really?”

Xavier had never heard of such technology.  
He stared at the brochure in disbelief.  
The manufacturer claimed the headband could transmit signals straight into the brain.  
The idea sounded so revolutionary he struggled to believe it.

> Boost your efficiency with the smart headband!

The advertisement showed a young man solving a math puzzle with a focused expression.  
Xavier noticed people rarely smiled in ads — society preferred ambitious, emotionless types.

“Would you like to try one, sir?” the clerk asked.  
“We have five basic models. May I know the color of your badge?”

Xavier showed him the silver tag.  
The clerk’s eyes widened in shock.  
Xavier knew he didn’t look like a typical silver citizen — his outdated clothes, subdued demeanor, and exhausted expression made him look like a hopeless plastic‑tag.

“I’m sorry if I offended you,” the clerk said.  
He was likely apologizing for his assumptions.

*People have grown so used to arrogance that politeness seems like weakness,* Xavier thought.  
*I must confuse him.*

“Go on,” he said neutrally.

While the clerk went to the storage room, another customer entered — a middle‑aged woman in a grey suit, short hair, and an OR headband.  
Cold blue eyes, narrow nose, lipstick.  
A silver badge on a gold chain above her neckline.

She scanned the room, then marched to the counter and snapped:  
“Step aside, sir. I’m in a hurry.”

Xavier rolled his eyes.  
“Get in line, ma’am. You’ll have to wait.”

The answer stunned her.  
A man who dared oppose her had to be a plastic‑tag — and the worst kind.  
No one with self‑respect would walk around in baggy trousers and a faded shirt.  
Bronze employees couldn’t — they’d be fired.  
Silver and gold wouldn’t — superiority was their lifestyle.  
Even syndicate members cared about appearances.  
Plastic‑tags with hope of advancement dressed better too.

“I’m not in the mood for your insolence,” she said and turned to the returning clerk.  
“You there! I need service immediately.”

“Of course, ma’am. I’ll call my colleague to assist you.”

“Excuse me?”

She clearly expected a different reaction.  
But the clerk couldn’t prioritize her over Xavier — store policy treated both customers equally.  
He tried to solve it by calling the other clerk.

“Absolutely not,” she snapped.  
“You will serve me. This man”—she pointed at Xavier—“will wait!”

The clerk froze.  
He couldn’t comply without offending the first silver citizen.

“I’m sorry, ma’am,” he said. “This gentleman was here first. My colleague will gladly assist you.”

“I don’t care about your colleague,” she growled.  
“Do you see my badge? Should I shove it in your face? This man waits or you throw him out. He was rude.”

“Ma’am, I…”

“Are you defying me? One word from me and you’re back on the street. Your incompetence is obvious.”

Sweat formed on the clerk’s forehead.  
If she filed a complaint, he’d lose his job — and his bronze badge — regardless of who was right.  
No corporation would hire him again.

There was no escape.  
He couldn’t expect help from Xavier — what silver citizen would swallow such humiliation?

“Are you deaf?” she pressed.

“No, ma’am. I just…”

He couldn’t explain who Xavier was — revealing someone’s badge status to another customer was illegal.  
The law was meant to protect plastic‑tags from humiliation, but since Xavier had hidden his badge again, the clerk had no way out.

Xavier was trapped too.  
If he backed down, he’d look suspicious enough for the clerk to call the police — accusing him of forging his badge.  
It would be a nuisance for Xavier, but the clerk would lose everything.

*If this situation must end,* Xavier thought, *let it end with one less silver tyrant.*

He turned, unbuttoned his shirt, and stared at the woman.

“It seems you’re trying to insult me, ma’am. I’ll need a moment of your valuable time.”
