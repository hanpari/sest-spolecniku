# I Need Your Explicit Consent

*Find the next number in the sequence: 2, 4, 8, …*

“Sixteen,” Xavier growled.

“Very good,” the nurse praised him. “Shall we increase the difficulty?”

“Go ahead.”

A surge of venom shot through the imaginary needles under his skin.

*Which number is not prime: 2, 3, 23, 27?*

“Twenty‑seven.”

*When I was fifteen,* Xavier thought, *it was easier to endure.*  
Sweat drenched his body. Every time he had to focus on a question, he bit down harder on the gag.

“Shall we proceed?”

The girl’s brown eyes pleaded with him not to. She knew what would follow and hoped for mercy. When she earned her bronze badge and congratulated herself for climbing higher on the social ladder, she probably hadn’t realized her new position involved torturing patients.

Xavier was certain that was why she’d been assigned here. Corporations had no use for soft hearts. Empathy and altruism were obsolete idols. Real jobs had become scarce after automation, and to join the chosen few, one’s character mattered more than skill.

The mere fact she had a bronze badge meant she’d proven sufficient moral toughness. Corporations cast their recruitment nets in schools, and their methods were rarely straightforward. The entrance exams had both visible and hidden parts — sometimes ending in injuries or deaths.

“An unfortunate accident,” he muttered through the gag. “You’ve had one yourself, haven’t you?”

“Please focus, Mr Valehart,” she said, convinced he was rambling.

“It’s usually a race, a test of endurance. A little push, a slip, a fall — off a slope, off a cliff. Am I wrong?”

“Mr Valehart, please. You have a number sequence before you.”

“Footage no one reviews. Congratulations from the organizers. Parents’ complaints ignored. Isn’t that right?”

Before the young nurse could grasp what he meant, Doctor Blackheart spoke:  
“I think he’s perfectly lucid. Proceed to the next level, nurse.”

The venom turned into liquid fire.

*Can a triangle be formed with sides 3, 4, and 10?*

“No,” Xavier spat the word and bit down again.

*Table salt — KCl or NaCl?*

“NaCl.”

“Excellent,” the doctor said, stepping to the controls. “I wager we can raise the stakes.”

“Doctor,” the older nurse objected, “we don’t need to go higher.”

“We don’t need to,” he smiled, “but we can — if we have the patient’s consent.”

“Couldn’t that cause cardiac arrest?” the younger nurse asked.

“There’s always some risk. But as they say: hard training, easy battle. It’s in our interest to test Mr Valehart thoroughly if we’re to invest time and capital in him. Don’t worry, nurse. He’ll agree with me, won’t you?”

“Bastard!”

“I need your explicit consent,” the doctor reminded calmly. “Clear and unambiguous. A simple yes, Mr Valehart. And no tricks, please.”

“Or perhaps…” the young nurse began. It was obvious she wanted to warn him. A yes meant pain; a no meant failure. If she finished her sentence, the doctor would likely have her removed from payroll.

“Be quiet, hen,” Xavier snapped. “Don’t meddle in things you don’t understand. If he wants to continue, let him. Come on, doctor — I’m waiting. We both know what this is.”

“As you wish.”

After a brief hesitation, the doctor stood behind the control panel.  
“Do I have your consent to proceed?”

“By all means.”

“Yes or no?”

“Yes.”

The lever moved up a notch. Xavier’s body arched violently. The pain was so unbearable that darkness swallowed him.

“Mr Valehart, please read the question and answer. On the Moon…”

*On the Moon, will you be lighter or heavier?*

He focused on the letters dissolving in the haze.

“Heavier… I mean lighter.”

“I need a clear answer. Once more, please.”

“Lighter, damn it.”

“Once more. One word only.”

“Lighter!”

“You’re starting to make mistakes,” the doctor said kindly. “Elementary physics. Very easy questions. Do you have trouble with physics?”

“Not in the least,” Xavier hissed. “Continue!”

“Army or corporation?”

Before he could answer, Xavier froze. That wasn’t on the test screen. It was a trick — a probe to see which faction he belonged to. He ignored it and focused on the monitor.

*A balanced lever. One kilogram on one side. What weight on the other to keep balance?*

“One kilogram.”

“Do you wish to continue, Mr Valehart?”

“No.”

“Are you sure?”

“Go to hell!”

“So yes or no?”

“Yes.”

“I’ll take that as informed consent,” the doctor said and pushed the lever higher.  
Xavier could only blame himself for falling for such a cheap trick.

“Doctor!”

With the nurses’ terrified cries, Xavier plunged into agony. It was more than he could bear — impossible to answer anything.

Neural induction caused intense pain without leaving marks and, in mild doses, didn’t endanger life. But that didn’t mean the human brain could tolerate it. When Xavier came to, the doctor and both nurses were busy reviving him. The straps on his wrists had been loosened, and a fresh patch covered the injection site on his arm.

Whatever he thought of corporate employees, he had to admit they were professionals. Still, he didn’t rush to show he was conscious; instead, he considered his next move.

Doctor Blackheart, though eager for cooperation, had gone too far. No one would blame Xavier for refusing him later. Perhaps the doctor had his own motives — to assert dominance, to instill submission, especially after Xavier had disrupted his plans several times.

But that seemed unlikely. If the Game held secrets players weren’t meant to know, the doctor might be pursuing his own agenda. *Is pain the key to increasing my compatibility?*

Neural stimulation was considered advanced technology, sometimes recommended for developing the nervous system. Unbelievably, there were procedures akin to torture for which corporate princes and princesses paid fortunes — just to heighten their reflexes.

“How do you feel?” the doctor asked when Xavier opened his eyes. “For a moment we feared we’d lost you.”

“Was it necessary?”

Though his words were muffled by blood and swollen lips, the doctor nodded.  
A certain understanding passed between them.

“Very necessary. I’d advise you to register for the Game as soon as possible,” he said. “With such a promising start, it would be a shame to waste your opportunity. Few can boast such an excellent… ehm, compatibility rate.”

The euphemism *compatibility rate* sounded like mockery.

“So we’re done here?”

“As far as I’m concerned, yes,” the doctor smiled. “The nurse will run a few more tests, but the essential part is complete.”

“Hm.”

With a cold nod he forced himself to make, Xavier turned to the nurses.  
“You see what you’ve chosen. Believe me, reaching silver will cost you more… Please, return my identifier.”

The older nurse handed him the silver badge with a sour look.  
“Don’t think we can’t guess how you got yours. If you don’t work for the corporations, the alternatives are worse.”

Without comment — the doctor was listening — Xavier hung the badge around his neck.

A bitter déjà vu struck him. The same scenario as twenty years ago.  
Being chosen among thousands flattered him. The crowd of eager applicants was real, as was the doctor’s interest. He hadn’t thought of his insomnia once. And as for his test results—

“You were right, doctor,” the young nurse said eagerly, desperate to prove loyalty after her earlier mistake. “Mr Valehart has surpassed all previous candidates.”

“As expected. We’re proud of you, Xavier.”

*Of course,* Xavier thought. *Playing the tune of exceptionalism.*

To surpass others, to climb higher, to become better — a society built on division had perfected competition.

Only a fool would reject a new opportunity. Xavier knew his silver badge was pure luck, a coincidence rather than merit. People of his background were grateful for bronze, if they didn’t stagnate at the plastic tier — citizens whose contribution was so negligible they weren’t even entitled to renewal vaccines.

The problem was that no one could keep their rank forever. Each year, artificial intelligence recalculated the point system and reassigned citizen identifiers.

Though the criteria were secret, most gold and silver citizens were families tied to corporations or their long‑term senior employees. Others included politicians, officials, doctors, lawyers, high‑ranking officers — and members of syndicates who could maintain their status through artificial positions that generated taxable income.

Children were temporarily outside the system; at fifteen they received a colored Candidate badge, which they kept until leaving school. If they failed to become useful by societal standards, they ended up with a plastic badge and a few years of life until their last vaccination expired.

Women with children under twelve were exceptions. For men, the only path to bronze was military or other hazardous service — limited by age, so men over forty were rare among plastic holders.

Even women with plastic badges faced forced sterilization after their second child to conserve resources.
