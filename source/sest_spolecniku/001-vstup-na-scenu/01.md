# Don’t Tell Me the Syndicate Is Recruiting Players?

> When they can’t see the chains, they believe they’re free.  
> — *Mijena Adloff*

More than anything else, the **Game Centre** resembled a bargain sale under siege — crowds pressing in, desperate to get inside, driven by the faint hope of a discount.

Staring at the queue stretching across the entire car park like an endless serpent, **Xavier Valehart** felt an overwhelming urge to turn on his heel and go home.

The man at the end of the line noticed his hesitation and waved cheerfully.  
“No worries, friend. Looks terrifying, but it moves quickly.”

“Really?” Xavier eyed him with deep suspicion. “What does ‘quickly’ mean?”

“You walk in, and you walk out. That quick.”

“Come again?”

The stranger didn’t look like a comedian, but he clearly possessed a peculiar sense of humour. A broad‑shouldered fellow with a kindly expression, dressed in blue jeans and a checkered flannel shirt. The round spectacles completed the impression of a harmless supporting character from a cosy TV drama.

“I may look simple,” Xavier said, “but I can still count. And unless I’m mistaken—” he pointed at the leaflet in the man’s hand “—doesn’t it say every Player undergoes a thorough examination? That doesn’t sound like something you just stroll through.”

His remark earned a ripple of laughter from the people around them. Before he could congratulate himself, a young woman with dreadlocks and a neck covered in blue tattoos waved at him impatiently.

“Please just get in line and stop being clever. The exit’s right there. See for yourself.”

With the comforting thought that he could leave at any moment, Xavier obeyed, stepped behind the bearded man, and watched the gate marked **EXIT** flashing in angry red. A steady stream of disappointed faces poured out.

“Well? Told you,” the bearded man chuckled, entirely unoffended by Xavier’s lack of trust. “I wanted to give up too, but our colleague here talked me out of it.”

He must have meant the tattooed woman. She didn’t turn around, so Xavier had time to study the rest of her neck. What he’d taken for a beaver was in fact a swaddled infant, with the inscription *My Little Poppet*. Judging by the sling under her chin, she was carrying the real‑life model. From the glimpse under her shirt, the baby was currently feeding.

“It wouldn’t be so bad,” she muttered sourly, “if some idiots learned how to lose.”

Xavier wouldn’t have known what she meant if a group hadn’t joined the queue behind him — three young men in leather jackets and military haircuts. They caused a stir by shoving aside several weaker participants, ignoring the protests.

“There’s loads of them,” the bearded man whispered. “They keep trying to get in again and again. Heard some are here for the tenth time.”

“Got a problem?” one of the youths asked. He, too, had a tattoo under his chin — not a baby, but a skull crowned with laurel. “No rule says how many times we can try.”

“No,” the woman said acidly, “it just makes you look pathetic. If it weren’t for people like you, I’d be inside already.”

“Or you could’ve stayed home and looked after the brat,” the youth sneered.

“I don’t recall giving you permission to call me ‘love’, sunshine,” she shot back.

The situation was about to blossom into a full‑blown argument, so Xavier quickly turned to the bearded man.

“What’s this about? Why would people try more than once?”

“Because we haven’t cracked the trick,” the youth answered before the bearded man could inhale. “But we think we’re close.”

“How close?”

Both the bearded man and the tattooed mother perked up.

“Oh, come on,” the youth smirked. “Information costs money. Interested?”

“How much?”

“Ten thousand.” He waved his phone. “Pay up and everything we recorded is yours.”

“Sure,” the woman snorted. “I won’t see anything I haven’t seen online. It’s always the same.”

“If you don’t want it, don’t buy it. And you two, gentlemen?” He turned to Xavier and the bearded man. “I’ll give you a discount.”

Xavier declined, but noticed the bearded man hesitating.

“Don’t be ridiculous,” the woman scolded him. “Everyone knows what happens. You walk in, you walk down a corridor, and you’re out. You’re not compatible, you’re not a Player. It’s written right there on the leaflet.”

“Excuse me.”

Without waiting for permission, Xavier borrowed the leaflet and skimmed it again.

> **Compatibility**  
> At the **Game Centre**, we test your eligibility using a quick method.  
> Although our technology is constantly improving, we can currently grant access to the Game only to a limited number of applicants.

“Well, well,” Xavier murmured. He’d imagined the Game was yet another attempt by international corporations to squeeze citizens dry, but here he’d been wrong. Demand clearly exceeded supply.

“If you walk down the corridor and end up outside, you’re not a Player,” the woman explained. “It’s that simple. No examination, nothing. Goodbye and good luck.”

“That’s rubbish,” the youth objected. “With that attitude, no one would ever get in. They’re testing us. Only those who truly want to succeed get inside.”

“Why not,” the bearded man mused. “I worked for corporations. They’re always inventing tricks to test our loyalty, endurance, and so on. Once—”

“If you’re right,” the woman cut in, “someone would have succeeded by now. I’ve never heard of anyone getting in on a second attempt.”

“We have, haven’t we, lads?” the youth grinned at his friends. They laughed like an obedient pack. “But as I said, information isn’t free.”

“You’ve heard unverified rumours,” the woman said. “I’ve read tons of those. A friend of a friend said he knew someone who—blah blah. Second‑hand stories. We know how that goes. Isn’t that right?”

She turned to Xavier this time, since the bearded man had proven a weak ally. Her healthy scepticism appealed to him, so he nodded.

“It seems,” he said, “that people really are trying to get inside.”

“Hmph.” She snorted. “If I knew whom to bribe, I’d have done it ages ago. The problem is, the corporations keep everything under wraps. No one knows what’s behind the Game. The only thing we know is that it’s huge. You can supposedly earn a lot of money. The sooner you get in, the better.”

“Wait!” Xavier froze. “Did you say money? I thought we were talking about a virtual game?”

“Of course money,” she snapped, pointing at her sleeping child. “Or do you think I’d bother standing here for fun? This is the Game, dear sir!”

“That explains everything,” the youth sneered.

Waiting in line was dull and tedious. The technology itself must have been a constant topic of speculation.

“That’s right,” the bearded man said. “Getting into the Game is a guaranteed ticket to a well‑paid position in any corporation. You can take your pick.”

“Sounds like winning the lottery,” Xavier muttered.

“Say what you want,” the bearded man huffed. “And why are you here?”

“Insomnia,” Xavier replied simply, prompting a wave of laughter up and down the queue.

“Yes, insomnia,” he repeated calmly. “Don’t ask why — AICHAT sent me.”

“AICHAT? Mate,” the youth blurted, “I thought that buggy relic was used only by old folks on their deathbed.”

“Well, I’m not the youngest,” Xavier said. “Listening to you, it seems I’m wasting my time, no? I’m not planning to work for corporations. And you three certainly aren’t either. Don’t tell me the Syndicate is recruiting Players?”

The laughter died instantly. Everyone knew what the trio was involved in, but no one dared say it aloud.

“I don’t know what you’re talking about, sir.”

Xavier tapped the frame of his smart glasses. “Sure. AICHAT may be an outdated AI, but it can still recognise gang tattoos. Do you three really think the Syndicate recruits street thugs?”

“Who says we’re street thugs?”

“If you’re not, hide your tattoos. And now kindly clear off. I don’t like your manners.”

People must have thought Xavier was a dangerous lunatic provoking strangers for no reason. They cautiously stepped away from him.

“We won’t let that slide, sir!”

“Oh, I think you will,” Xavier said, flashing the silver badge marking him as a second‑class citizen.

“Back to my insomnia,” he said once the youths had left, cursing under their breath.

“What if they come back?” the woman with the baby asked. “You shouldn’t have shown that cursed thing. They don’t like people like you. I don’t either.”

“I understand,” Xavier shrugged. Under normal circumstances he would have kept quiet, but he’d done the public a favour. No one felt safe around the youths. Now some kept their distance from him as well.

“You still risked more than you should have,” she continued.

“I’d say they weren’t nearly as dangerous as they’d like to think,” Xavier said.

“I’d say they knew exactly what they were doing,” she replied. “Be careful. I know these types. They’ll wait for you somewhere without cameras.”

Xavier waved dismissively. “Do you, madam, have any explanation why AICHAT sent me here?”

“No idea. But speaking of insomnia — you’ve got lovely dark circles under your eyes. You look like an escaped patient.”

“I might have a theory,” the bearded man said, apparently impressed by Xavier’s uncompromising stance — and the silver badge. “You see, back when I worked in the corporate world, I learned to prepare thoroughly. In corporations, it’s always life or death. Figuratively.”

He laughed, but no one joined him. That didn’t stop him; his life experience had earned him an audience. For most people, working for a corporation was an unattainable dream.

“So,” he continued, as if giving a presentation, “as you know, only people capable of connecting to the World can play the Game. How the World was created and how it works remains a mystery and a subject of speculation, which we don’t have time for now.”

Another smile. Again, no reaction.

“Everyone knows aliens are behind it,” someone said. “I can share an article.”

“Of course,” the bearded man replied dryly. “If it’s on the internet, who would doubt it? Still, I’d like to stick to facts.”

“Laugh all you want,” the alien‑believer muttered, ready to defend himself before anyone attacked. “We don’t have computers powerful enough to create the World.”

“I’m not arguing,” the bearded man said — and added with mild disdain: “If everyone knows something, there’s no point repeating it. I wanted to talk about things not everyone knows.”

“Then talk,” Xavier encouraged him. “We’ve got a long queue ahead. We’ll manage even the alien part.”

“I didn’t say they’re aliens. That’s exactly—”

“Oh, please shut up,” the woman with the baby snapped. “I want to hear about the insomnia.”

“It’s not about insomnia,” the bearded man said. “It’s about a whole range of symptoms shown by potential Players. People with depression, insomnia, lack of focus, fatigue, or manic‑depressive episodes have a higher chance.”

“That’s most of us,” the woman snorted, and the others agreed. “We’re all stuck in the same mess.”

“True,” the bearded man nodded. “But getting into the Game supposedly cures insomnia. And much more,” he added meaningfully. “After all, you enter the Game in your sleep.”

“In sleep?” Xavier scoffed. “So I just need to fall asleep? I’ve been trying for a month.”

“Maybe it’s artificial sleep,” the bearded man suggested. “I’ve heard Players fall asleep within five minutes of connecting.”

“That would be nice,” Xavier said. “But I think I’m wasting my time here. It was nice meeting you all, but I should go.”

As soon as he turned to leave, several voices urged him to stay.

“Don’t go,” the woman grabbed his sleeve. “You’ve waited this long — give it a moment. You might score. Silver badge or not!”

Feeling he’d disappoint the expectations suddenly placed on him, Xavier returned to his spot behind the bearded man — who immediately resumed his enthusiastic monologue about corporate life.

His corporate career, as it turned out, had been pitiful. He’d attended a training course to become a store manager for an electronics shop; the corporation automated it a year later. During that time, he’d had a few chances to visit the higher management centre, which he described with enthusiasm worthy of something far grander. Then came the farewell and the temporary status of a third‑class citizen.

Xavier knew well that the world was divided between those who admired corporations and those who despised them. The bearded man belonged to the former. Xavier to the latter.
