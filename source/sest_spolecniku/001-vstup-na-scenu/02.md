# I’m Here Because of Insomnia

When the long wait finally ended, **Xavier Valehart** stepped through the turnstile into the vestibule — the place he already knew would lead him to the corridor with the exit. Above the door glowed a red countdown timer, giving each visitor exactly one minute to figure out how to reach the **Game’s Admissions Office**.

*Strange,* Xavier thought. The moment the turnstile clicked shut behind him, a wave of nausea washed over him. The corridor and its walls blurred as if he’d just stepped off a violently spinning carousel.

He blinked rapidly, trying to dispel the black specks dancing before his eyes. Whatever was causing the dizziness, it grew worse with every second.

“To hell with this!”

He tried not to panic and hurried toward the exit, desperate for fresh air. But the door remained locked. The timer still showed more than thirty seconds.

“Let me out!” he shouted. He had never suffered from claustrophobia, yet now he felt as if a coffin lid were closing over him.

*Emergency exit,* he thought. *A key. A lever. Anything.*  
He scanned the walls and caught a faint inscription in the corner of his vision: **Press Here!** Beneath it, half‑sunken into the wall, was a nearly invisible button.

He slammed his fist into it and burst through the door as it swung open.  
The moment he left the dim corridor, the nausea evaporated.

“Never again,” he gasped, leaning against the nearest wall to steady himself.

“Quite the dramatic entrance,” someone remarked. “Please remember to save this data for later review. How do you feel, sir? Could you describe what happened?”

Only then did Xavier realise he had actually reached the **Game’s interview room**. A warm surge of satisfaction washed over him — something he hadn’t felt in a long time. There was a certain sweetness in succeeding where most people failed.

“Insomnia,” he croaked. “Insomnia. I’m here because of insomnia. Can you help me? Without pills, without drugs?”

“Insomnia?”

This time, his words did not provoke laughter.

Three figures in white coats stood before him, each looking equally startled by his sudden arrival. The coats — and the faint air of superiority so common among medical staff — made it clear they were doctors and nurses.

“Insomnia is one of the syndromes we’re supposed to monitor,” noted the younger nurse — almost a girl — wearing a metal dog tag around her neck, the equivalent of Xavier’s ID card. The older nurse, a woman in her forties with curly hair and an unremarkable face, kept her tag hidden beneath her pale blue blouse, but Xavier was certain she and the doctor were close to reaching the **silver tier**.

“So you came because of insomnia,” the doctor asked with professional interest. Judging by the red marks on his face, he had been resting his head in his hands — perhaps even dozing off. “And why do you think the Game can help you?”

“AICHAT told me to come,” Xavier replied.

Part of him was curious, perhaps even tempted by the promise of a new fantastical World, but he refused to give the corporate staff the satisfaction of seeing his interest. He leaned against the door with his arms crossed.

“Was that a mistake?” he asked challengingly.

“AICHAT? The artificial intelligence?”

“Of course.” Xavier tapped his smart glasses. They were old, yes, but he kept them more out of sentiment than practicality.

“AICHAT and insomnia,” the doctor murmured. “An intriguing combination. Has it ever occurred to you that AICHAT might be causing your sleep problems?”

“Should it? I’ve used AICHAT for years.”

“Certainly,” the doctor smiled and stepped closer, extending a hand as if to shake his — though a professional like him would never touch a stranger without gloves. The lingering fear of the last viral pandemic still clung to medical staff, even if the public had long abandoned masks and distancing.

The doctor leaned in with unsettling intimacy.

“Some might say the public deserves a better model. You know… our tools don’t always perform as they should,” he whispered conspiratorially. “Didn’t AICHAT receive a major software update recently?”

Xavier understood the implication immediately. AICHAT *had* updated recently — and his insomnia had worsened.

And more importantly: the corporation behind AICHAT had been acquired by a competitor. If Xavier was right, his troubles might be the result of the new owner’s meddling.

He had been wearing his glasses out of sheer stubbornness lately. Then again, he wasn’t doing anything willingly these days — not with sleep deprivation gnawing at his sanity.

“So you think I need a new model?”

“I never said that,” the doctor smiled. “Such a statement would land me — and my employer — in court.”

“And who *is* your employer?” Xavier asked.

“That’s no secret,” the doctor said. “The Game isn’t developed by a single corporation. Many companies and individuals contribute. You could say the entire planet has a hand in it.”

*Vague,* Xavier noted. Not a single concrete name.

“In truth,” the doctor continued, “you’d struggle to find a corporation that *doesn’t* have a stake in the Game. Believe me, friend — you won’t find a better ticket into the upper leagues. Work hard and you’ll reach silver within a year.”

Xavier smirked and held up his **silver ID card**.

“Try harder, doctor. I’m here because of insomnia.”

Ignoring the doctor’s embarrassment, Xavier sat down opposite the desk and gestured for him to follow. The older nurse frowned disapprovingly but said nothing. Clearly, none of them had reached silver yet.

“Your name, sir? Date of birth? Address? Occupation?”

“One moment,” Xavier interrupted. “The doctor still owes me an answer. My insomnia troubles me far more than access to the Game — however magnificent it may be.”

The nurse flinched at the contempt in his voice.

“Some people would kill to sit where you’re sitting,” she said pointedly.

“And some would kill to stand where *you* are,” Xavier replied. “Do you know how many nurses are among citizen‑candidates?”

“That’s enough,” the doctor cut in, motioning for her to stop.

“If I understand correctly,” he continued, “you hope that entering the Game will cure your insomnia.”

“Yes.”

“And perhaps it will,” the doctor said. “But we can’t guarantee anything. Passing the compatibility test doesn’t mean you’ll enter the Game itself.”

“So?”

“Enter the Game and your problems may disappear. Fail, and you’ll need to seek help elsewhere. Are you still interested?”

“And if I say no?”

The hungry looks the three of them gave him made it obvious they wanted him more than he wanted them.

*What’s going on here?* he wondered.

“I won’t waste your time,” he said, pretending to rise. “I’ll think about it.”

“You have nothing to lose,” the doctor said quickly. “Please don’t rush. It’s unlikely another potential Player will appear — at least not in the next half hour.”

Even a fool would notice how badly he wanted Xavier to stay.

“Fine,” Xavier said. “Tell me what you’re offering.”

“Very well,” the doctor exhaled. “First—”

“First,” Xavier interrupted, “tell me how I even got here. That experience wasn’t pleasant. I felt dizzy. The room spun. And suddenly a door appeared that wasn’t there before.”

“Oh, it *was* there,” the doctor said. “But if you weren’t compatible with the Game, you’d never have found it. Look.”

With a smug smile, he showed Xavier a security recording. Coincidentally, it featured the bearded man who had entered the test corridor before him.

“This man,” the doctor explained, “has a certain affinity. I’d estimate around fifty percent. Quite good compared to other candidates. His misfortune is that he came too early.”

“Too early?”

“In a sense. We expect the required affinity threshold to decrease over time as the Game’s interface adapts to our conditions.”

“So even aliens need time to fine‑tune things,” Xavier smirked.

No one laughed.

“You’ve read online speculation,” the doctor said. “I assure you, the Game’s technology will exceed your expectations. I know no one who entered and wasn’t amazed.”

“I played a few virtual games when I was fifteen,” Xavier said lightly. “I assume the world has advanced since then.”

“You’ll soon see reality surpass your expectations. The Game represents the cutting edge of neural research. You’ll enter a new world.”

He spoke with unmistakable envy.

“So you never got in, doctor?”

“Sadly, no. That man—” he pointed at the bearded figure “—has a better chance than I do. Notice how he stopped exactly where the entrance to this office is hidden.”

“Mysterious affinity?” Xavier said dryly.

“Exactly. Above the door is an NT transmitter masking the entrance. When you stepped into its field, you felt nauseous. But you pushed through. Look!”

On the recording, the bearded man spun in confusion like a pigeon hitting a window, then bolted for the exit in terror.

*Greater willpower?* Xavier thought. *I panicked and stumbled in by accident. The poor fellow just had bad luck.*

“So those people running in circles outside — do they have a chance?”

“Not at all,” the doctor said. “Most walk through without noticing anything. But he might succeed if he returns. You know him, don’t you?”

There was a strange eagerness in his voice.

Xavier realised the doctor hadn’t shown him the recording by accident. The monitors displayed feeds from all around the office. If the doctor had seen Xavier talking to the bearded man, perhaps he was hinting at something.

Corporations loved measurable metrics. It wasn’t impossible that the staff were rewarded for recruiting potential Players.

Xavier had little sympathy for corporate employees, but he sensed that doing the doctor a favour might be useful.

“I know him,” Xavier lied smoothly. “Shall I give him a message?”
